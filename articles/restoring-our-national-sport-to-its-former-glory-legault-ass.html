"Restoring our national sport to its former glory": Legault assembles task force as homegrown talent numbers dwindle

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Jan 19, 2022

When the Chicago Blackhawks played the Montreal Canadiens on December 9, Quebec hockey fans had a reason to celebrate... even if the Habs lost.

The Chicago victory represented goaltender Marc-André Fleury's 500th win in the NHL, a milestone that has only been eclipsed by two other players: fellow French Canadians Patrick Roy and Martin Brodeur.

Although Fleury deserved to be celebrated, the evening symbolized a disappointing reality for his home province: Quebec hockey is in decline.

At 37 years old, Fleury represents the end of a lineage of great Quebec goaltenders that started with Georges Vézina, whom the award given to the best goaltender NHL is named after (ironically, Fleury is the most recent recipient of the coveted Vézina trophy). This season, five Quebec-born goalies have suited up for at least one game; Fleury is the sole full-time starter among them.

Even more staggering is that only 60 of the 1000 players to have played at least one game so far this season were born in Quebec. By comparison, 192 NHL players, nearly a fifth of the NHL population, were born in Ontario.

The number of Quebec-born players in the NHL has been in steady decline over the past two decades. While there were 71 players in 2011-2012, the 2001-2002 season featured 103 players at a time where there were two less teams in the league.

The issue of player development in Quebec has become so apparent that Premier Francois Legault has taken notice, and taken action. In November 2021, Legault announced a plan to bolster the province's hockey development program with the intention of producing more world-class talent. He created the Quebec Committee on Hockey Development, a 15-member committee comprised of former NHL and women's hockey stars, to submit recommendations on how to get more young Quebecers playing hockey.

It can take years or even decades to make improvements that will show up on hockey's biggest stage, so the youth level is where the committee will have to do the most work.

In the 1019 region, there are currently 1,432 registered minor hockey players between the four minor associations: Vaudreuil-Dorion, Hudson-Rigaud-Saint Lazare (HRS), Ile Perrot, and Soulanges. The associations' executives agree with Legault's mandate that something needs to be done.

"I don't know if the number of players in the area has gone down in the area, but the of hockey quality has certainly become diluted," said Sylvain Sevigny, president of the HRS association.

Sevigny explained that the sport-study program at high schools like Chêne Bleu in Pincourt have become a popular option for kids and families because they don't play on weekends. However, he thinks the dispersal of top talent between two minor league circuits has hurt player development. The division means that the top leagues are no longer best-on-best, making competition inferior to the Ontario minor league system.

"Our AAA league is actually closer to AA-calibre because of all the players in sport-study programs. It pains me to say it as president of the minor hockey association, but we can't compete with player development in the school system. They're on the ice every day, and the fact the sport is tied to academic success incentivizes kids to do well in school, too," Sevigny said.

COVID-19 has also had an impact on minor hockey in the region.

"It's true that we've seen numbers decrease over the years, and the pandemic hasn't helped," said Christina Fazio, president of the Ile Perrot minor hockey association.

"I've noticed it with my younger one's age group. Many of them missed playing in the novice program, so there is a major gap between the kids that continued to play over the pandemic and the ones who didn't," added Sevigny.

While Legault's push to encourage hockey in the province may appear to be positive from a hockey fan's perspective, the Premier's actions have come under criticism. Specifically, several respected voices in the Quebec hockey community have called out the decision to nominate an all-French-Canadian, all-white committee because it excludes the province's minorities.

"Legault spoke about hockey being 'our national sport' and he wasn't talking about it being Canada's national sport. When he talked about developing more Quebec players for the NHL, he wasn't thinking about Alex Killorn, Marco Scandella or Michael Matheson," wrote long-time Gazette hockey columnist Pat Hickey on the number of Anglo-Quebecers in the NHL.

"Hockey cannot survive if minorities don 't get interested. How could you be attracting minorities to come and play hockey if there 's no representation of them in the group that 's trying to do so? " asked Georges Laraque, a legendary NHL tough guy and the son of Haitian immigrants, in an interview with CBC. The provincial government's press release highlighted diversity as one of the concerns it plans to address.

The Quebec Committee on Hockey Development will undoubtedly have this hockey-crazed province's undivided attention when it is expected to report back on April 1.
