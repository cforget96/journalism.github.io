Vaudreuil defers zoning change for places of worship in commercial areas

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Apr 27, 2022

Ahead of its public meeting on April 19, the city of Vaudreuil-Dorion planned to approve a zoning change to allow religious groups to occupy buildings in commercial zones. The rezoning effort came after the city's failed attempt in 2020 to rezone an area in its industrial park to allow for a mosque. But the item was removed and deferred to a later meeting at the last minute because the city received a valid request for a referendum from specific zone.

According to information obtained by The 1019 Report, the request for a referendum comes from the area around the intersection of St-Antoine and Chicoine, which is the location the Islamic Cultural Centre wanted to move into.

In the fall of 2020, the city's attempt to change the zoning to allow a mosque on the site of the former Sunny's restaurant in the municipality's industrial zone was pushed to a referendum after residents opposed to the move signed a register to block it. A total of 157 residents signed the register. A minimum of 59 signatures were needed to force the city to either hold the referendum or drop the proposed zoning change. The city cancelled the referendum after officials with the Islamic Cultural Centre of Vaudreuil-Soulanges abandoned its plan to move into the site.

"That was not the first time there has been an issue over the zoning of places of worship," Vaudreuil-Dorion Director General Oliver Van Neste said. "But it shows we needed to address the issue."

Property owners in the affected and neighbouring zones who oppose the amendments had until April 15 to register their objections with the city. Now, the eligible voters in the zone must sign the register for a referendum to be held.

"In this case, since the number of eligible voters is less than 25, at least 50% of the eligible voters must sign the register to force a referendum," explains the town's clerk, Jean St-Antoine.

According to the new bylaw, commercial areas along Highway 40, St. Charles Ave. and parts of De La Gare Blvd. and Harwood in the Dorion section of the city would permit religious activities, including houses of worship and cultural centres.

The fate of the zone requesting a referendum remains unclear, but the city stated that the new bylaws for places of worship will apply to zones where no applications were received.

The city held a public consultation on March 28 to explain the changes and to address questions. Only six residents attended. None opposed the zoning change at the time, said Van Neste.

"The residents who attended the consultation wanted to know more about which zones would be affected by the amendment. If there are people who want to oppose it, they were not present at the consultation," Van Neste explained.

Vaudreuil-Dorion's efforts reflect a need to adapt to a shifting demographic. The city recently received a grant of nearly $30,000 from the provincial Ministry of Immigration, Francization and Integration to develop strategies to address needs of a culturally and religiously diverse population. According to Van Neste, the funds will be primarily used for consulting.

The city does not have statistics to support the rise in diversity, but Van Neste says the changes are noticeable.

"We see it every day, be it our library clientele, at the pool, at our events," he said. "The demand to make changes does not only come from residents, either. City employees are asking for us to make adjustments to better serve the community."
