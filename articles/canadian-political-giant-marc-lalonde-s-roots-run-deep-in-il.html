Canadian political giant Marc Lalonde's roots run deep in Ile Perrot

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Jun 22, 2022

In 1674, Jean de Lalonde settled as a farmer on Francois-Marie Perrot's seigneurial state, making him one of the original inhabitants of the island. A few generations later in 1753, Antoine Lalonde moved onto his own land and built a two-storey stone house on Grande Anse, a bay which faces Lake St. Louis in what is today Notre Dame de l'Île Perrot.

For the next 269 years, seven generations of the Lalonde family farmed, lived and died on the island. Over the centuries, the old homestead has changed, but one thing has remained constant -- it is still the place current members of the family call home.

And as the island celebrates its 350th anniversary this summer, the generations of Lalondes are remembered. But there is one member -- the current patriarch of the family, who needs no introduction -- for his story extends from the shores of Île Perrot and at one time reached the highest levels of government in Canada.

First elected to the House of Commons in 1972 representing the riding of Outremont, Marc Lalonde was one of the most prominent members of Prime Minister Pierre Elliott Trudeau's cabinet. He held many posts during his 16-year tenure in Trudeau's administration, including Minister of National Health and Welfare, Minister of State, Minister of Justice and Attorney General, Minister of Energy, and Minister of Finance.

Lalonde was inducted into the Canadian Medical Hall of Fame for "A New Perspective on the Health of Canadians," a 1974 policy document that revolutionized Canadian health care. Simply referred to as the "Lalonde report," the document brought him international acclaim, and today his ideas are incorporated into health-care systems worldwide. The report is considered the first government document to take a holistic approach to health care, considering lifestyle and biomedical advancements as factors of national health.

Lalonde began working in politics as an advisor to Justice Minister Davie Fulton in 1959, during John Diefenbaker's tenure as Prime Minister. He briefly returned to Montreal to practice Law after his work for the Progressive Conservative Party, but in 1967 he was approached by the Liberals to work as an advisor for Lester B. Pearson.

"Pearson initially had me working on radio policy. He so was impressed with my work that he offered me to be the president of Radio-Canada," Lalonde remembered. "I was only 35 at the time and was not sure if I was ready for the role, so I declined the offer. I was expecting Pearson to be disappointed, but he came back and, to my surprise, offered me a job as an advisor."

Today, Lalonde is 92 years old and will celebrate his 93rd birthday next month. He retired from Canadian politics in 1984 after a 25-year tenure. He moved back to Ile Perrot full-time in 1989 after living in Ottawa and Cote-des-Neiges during his political career. He says he is proud to live in the home he grew up in.

"We lived a simple but happy life," Lalonde remembers of his childhood. "I enjoyed when my father would bring us to our sugar bush on the island to eat maple taffy."

In addition to working on the farm, Lalonde's father was a prominent member of the community, serving as mayor of the entire island for over 20 years. But Lalonde insists his own political career had little to do with father's involvement in local politics.

"My mother wanted me to be a priest, so she discouraged me from going into politics. She called it 'dirty,'" he recalled with a chuckle. "But that didn't apply to my father, of course."

Lalonde was made an Office of the Order of Canada in 1989. Although he had retired from politics, he continued to work, serving as a judge at the International Court of Justice and representing Canada in various trade disputes.

Through it all, Lalonde never lost sight of his goal to make Canada a better place.

"Practicing law certainly would have been more lucrative, but in my career, I always tried to use politics as an instrument of progress," Lalonde said.

Lalonde practiced international law until he retired two years ago at age 90. Even at this stage of his life, he likes to keep himself busy.

"I spend most of my time reading, communicating with old friends, maintaining the house and the garden, and spending time with family," he said.

In the twilight of his life, Lalonde would like one of his four children, ten grandchildren, and five great-grandchildren to keep the ancestral home in the family after him.

"We have deep roots on the island. There is a special attachment here that I hope will continue," he said.
