Pincourt unveils divisive tax bill weeks before election day: Why now?

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Nov 10, 2021

The owners of 82 houses in Pincourt have until Nov. 18 to decide how they want to pay an extra tax bill of between $5,000 and $6,000. Once the shock wore off, many found themselves asking why the short notice, and why they were being put in this financial squeeze just before last Sunday's municipal election.

"The confusion created by the poorly-worded notification letter we received has alarmed Pincourt citizens. The poor communication just before an election and during a pandemic recovery points to a city out of touch with its citizens," said Carole Reed, who is running as a Pincourt city councillor in District 6.

The notices detailed how much each homeowner would have to pay to cover their share of a $4.3-million loan bylaw to acquire a four-hectare wetland known as Rousseau forest, which is located between Olympic Boulevard and Duhamel Road. Taxpayers have the choice of paying the lump sum in full, or paying it over a 30-year period with a 5% interest rate.

The forested area, which had been slated for residential development, was the focus of a grassroots campaign spearheaded by a citizen-led group, Pincourt Vert. The movement culminated last year, when Pincourt council announced it would buy the 46 privately-owned lots. But it would come at a cost.

That cost was outlined in October 2020, when a tiered taxation scheme was unveiled that would force property owners closest to the forest to pay substantially more than taxpayers living further away from the green space. Few taxpayers objected at the time, and the plan was approved.

Now, however, this approach is being questioned. Some argue that there were few initial objections because the city did not advertise it properly.

"We had no idea what was going on. The city council has been unapproachable," said Shelagh McNally, acting president of Pincourt Vert, about the fact the city does not offer live broadcasts of city council.

Reed, who campaigned for the tax to be divided equitably in the fall of 2020, believes the mayor and council need to review the bylaw and discuss an amendment. If elected, Reed intends to send out a monthly bulletin to keep residents in the loop---something she feels has been missing from the administration during their term.

The municipal government, on the other hand, maintains that nothing about the bylaw has changed since they announced their mandate to acquire the woods, which is one of only two remaining wetlands in Pincourt.

"Everything has been done legally and correctly. The numbers have been the same the entire time, so many people are probably surprised because they forgot about it. Many people assumed that not voting in the referendum was the same as voting against the acquisition, which is not the case," explained Vicky Sauvé, director of communications for the city.

A point of confusion, however, is that the numbers have increased since last year. A 2020 projection had the annual tax at about $35 for residents that did not live close to the forest. The recent notice had the tax at $46. The city stated on their website that the amount is subject to change according to interest fluctuations, but residents only found out about the change when the recent bills were sent out.

"This a good example of their lack of transparency. There's no dialogue; why didn't they ask people what they wanted to do?" said McNally.

The timing of the notices---two weeks before the municipal election---is also being questioned.

"The provincial government has given the town until December 2 to finalize the loan application, and we only finished acquiring the lots two weeks ago," explained Sauvé.

As for why the city decided on taxation sectors, they see the forest as a benefit for those living around that may add value to the properties.

"Because of the acquisition, people with land that borders the forest have a guarantee of privacy. They will never have back-door neighbours, which is something people living on the border of the woods by Chêne-Bleu secondary school, for example, cannot say," Sauvé said.

But what the city considers an asset, McNally argues, precisely misses the point of preserving the woods.

"The wetland serves the entire community; it cools down all of Pincourt, not just the houses around it. This bylaw sets a dangerous precedent: it makes trees into a taxable luxury. The unpopularity of the tax bill will also discourage people to protect wooded areas in the future," McNally said.

Needless to say, the timing could not be worse for longtime Pincourt Mayor Yvan Cardinal and the city councillors who handled the case.

"This situation will definitely affect the election," said Sauvé. "But all you have to do is look at the facts to see it was all done properly."
