NDIP's scenic church one of the oldest in North America

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Jul 6, 2022

In the fall of 1760, a band of English soldiers sought refuge at an inn on the southeast tip of Ile Perrot on their way to take Montreal from the French. They didn't know it, but they were resting at the future site of Sainte Jeanne de Chantal Catholic church. Plans to build the church began in 1753, but the Seven Years' War halted the project because of a shortage of materials and labor.

Construction of the church was finally completed in 1774, making it one of the ten oldest surviving rural churches in North America. Built in a scenic location atop a bluff facing Beauharnois on the Saint Lawrence river, the 248-year-old church is a true historical gem of the 1019 region.

The combination of heritage and beauty makes the church an important patrimonial site; it was declared a historic monument by the Quebec Ministry of Cultural Affairs in 1961 and was named one of the Seven Wonders of Vaudreuil-Soulanges in 2008.

But the church has a complicated history, having gone through a number of iterations over the centuries. Lise Chartier, President of the Ile Perrot 350th anniversary committee, has penned two books on the history of the island, with another volume forthcoming. In her second volume, she dedicates over 70 pages to the history of the church.

"Ile Perrot became an established seigneury in 1672, and for several decades residents of the island often traveled to Sainte Anne de Bellevue by canoe to attend mass. A small presbytery was established close to Pointe du Moulin in 1743, but the chapel quickly became too small and residents complained that the church was not central enough. That's when plans for Sainte Jeanne de Chantal began," Chartier said.

Shortly after the church was constructed, the old cemetery was built in front of the church 1791. A second cemetery was built behind the church on the bluff in 1953, making it one of the only cemeteries of its kind in Quebec.

"I had heard that there was another scaffolded cemetery like it in Gaspésie, but I could never confirm it. The cemetery is very unique," said Chartier.

The church underwent renovations in the 19th century, including an enlargement in 1812 and the addition of a bell tower in 1842. When the tower collapsed in a gale in 1901, it was rebuilt with a new façade by Alcide Chaussé, the architect best known for his construction of the Egyptian-themed Empress theatre in NDG.

Sainte Jeanne de Chantal has already hosted several events during the 350th anniversary of Ile Perrot this summer. The next event at the patrimonial site will be the inauguration of a 350th anniversary garden on August 18 at 5 p.m.
