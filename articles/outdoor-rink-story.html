Outdoor rink story

By Christopher Forget · Jan 19, 2022

Bringing the goals home

As the pandemic forces people to stay in, more households are investing in backyard rinks

CHRISTOPHER FORGET

The 1019 Report

A return to isolation put a damper on the holiday the season, but homeowners across the 1019 region won't let that ruin their winter fun. Instead of going to public rinks to enjoy skating and playing outdoor hockey, many are bringing the annual tradition home.

"Five years ago, I was the only one in my neighbourhood with a backyard rink, but it's become more of a thing over the pandemic. Now there are four rinks in a row, neighbor-to-neighbour," said Sunil Peetush, a resident of Saint-Lazare.

Peetush's rink is immaculate, complete with two goalie creases, two blue lines, a red line, and of course, a Canadiens logo at centre ice. He said he made it for his kids, but over time, the rink has become a bit of a passion project.

Peetush even invented a contraption he calls a "broomboni" -- a few PVC pipes attached to a broom -- which coats the ice surface with a thin layer of hot water that freezes instantly, like a real zamboni.

"The ice will be as good as the work you're willing to put into it. My neighbours must think I'm crazy when I water it at one in the morning, but it's actually quite therapeutic. With the kids at home doing online school, maintaining the ice gives me some quiet time," Peetush said.

In Pincourt, Trevor Johnson has built a backyard rink of his own. The boards at his rink don two logos: the local Ile Perrot Riverains and the Boston Bruins.

"I know the Bruins one won't be very popular," Johnson, father of two, conceded.

"This is my second year doing it. The time off work allowed me to build the rink. Otherwise, I would have never had the time to do it. I added to it this year and plan on going bigger next year," he added.

But backdoor rinks do not come cheap. They can cost hundreds, even thousands of dollars, depending on size and quality.

"You're basically building a pool," explained Peetush. "You need to buy a liner to make a perimeter. The cheapest ones cost about $250 and it goes up from there. I have a kit that cost $3800, but it will last for another five or six years."

Johnson preferred not to specify how much he spent on his rink this year, but he recommends it to anyone who enjoys skating outdoors.

"The kids love it out there. Regardless of the cost, it's 100% worth it."
