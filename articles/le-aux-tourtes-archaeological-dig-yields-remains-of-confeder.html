Île aux Tourtes archaeological dig yields remains of Confederation-era home

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Jan 19, 2022

As part of the $100 million-dollar project to build a new Ile aux Tourtes bridge, the MTQ commissioned the archaeological firm Artefactual in the summer of 2021 to search the area for artefacts of historical significance. In an exclusive interview with The 1019 Report, the MTQ unveiled their findings.

The MTQ commissioned Artefactuel to conduct two separate inventory projects for a total of $100,000. The projects were conducted underwater south of Ile aux Tourtes and along the Senneville shore, respectively. The 2021 excavations built on previous projects commissioned by the MTQ archaeological projects in 2014 and 2019.

The underwater excavation, which included sonar coverage and archaeological dives, did not recover any items of substance. However, the firm made significant discoveries on Girdwood Island, a small island located just before Senneville under the Ile aux Tourtes bridge. This island gets its name from Dr. Gilbert Prout Girdwood and his wife, Fanny Merriman Blackwell Girdwood, who inhabited the island in the late 19th and early 20th century.

"The project uncovered the remains of the Girdwood residence, as well as the remains of greenhouses that were used to grow vines. The interventions allowed us to document the construction of the buildings and the operation of the greenhouses. Additionally, the excavation of the dump on Girdwood Island led to the discovery of numerous domestic artifacts from the 19th and 20th centuries," explained MTQ spokesperson Sarah Bensadoun. The artifacts will be deposited at the Laboratoire et la Réserve d 'archéologie du Québec (LRAQ) for preservation.

Dr. Girdwood was known, in his day, as a pioneer in medical education and radiography in Canada. Born in England, Girdwood came to Canada as a member of the British army during a diplomatic dispute with the United States at the time of the American Civil War. Girdwood decided to leave the British army to move to Canada permanently in 1864. Graduating from McGill College the following year, he became a prominent medical expert in Montreal, working as a professor at McGill College and medical staff officer for the Canadian militia.

While much of the Girdwoods' lives took place in the city, several archival photographs from the McCord Museum reveal that the couple would escape city life to spend time at their home in Senneville. In 1866, Girdwood photographed his wife at the ruins of Fort Senneville, which was burned to the ground during a battle with the Iroquois centuries prior, in 1691.

At this time, there will be no further archaeological activity planned in the area. However, Bensadoun says, if anything of geological, geotechnical, historical, or archaeological interest or value is discovered during the construction of the new bridge, the project could resume.

Kahnawake Council demands more prominent role in Ile aux Tourtes projects

CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report

Located at the confluence between the Ottawa river and the St. Lawrence river, the Ile aux Tourtes was a primary trade route and the primary maritime route between Kanasatake and Kahnawake. As such, the area is of great political and historical significance to the Mohawk people of the region. While the MTQ's recent archaeological projects have only found Euro-Canadian artefacts so far, the Mohawk people feel it is imperative that they take a leading role in the project.

"Our key concern is to maintain healthy environment with fish species and protection of endangered species. We want to stress a traditional mindset on how we should make decisions, but the provincial government seems to be more in the business of approving projects rather than thinking of the larger ethical long-term effects," said Kahnawake Council Chief Ross Montour.

Bensadoun explained that the MTQ proposed that the Council could attend the excavations as observers, but to Chief Montour, the title of observer, both for the archaeological project and the construction of the new bridge in general, is insufficient.

"Premier Legault's view is that indigenous people do not have the right to self-determination. We are invited to consultations, but the projects go through whether we agree with the decision or not. It gives the impression that we are only consulted for symbolic reasons, to check off a box," Montour adds.
