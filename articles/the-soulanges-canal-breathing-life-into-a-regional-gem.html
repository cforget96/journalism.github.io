The Soulanges canal: breathing life into a regional gem

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Aug 4, 2021

Vaudreuil-Soulanges residents have a historic landmark right in their back yard, and they may not even know it. It was the first canal in the world to have its entire passageway lit at night. That is just one claim to fame the Soulanges Canal holds title to.

Opened in 1899, the man-made waterway that traces its way along the north shore of the St. Lawrence River stretches for 23 kilometres -- from Pointe des Cascades to Coteaux Landing. Originally designed to bypass the rapids between Lake St. Louis and Lake St. Francis, it operated until 1959, when it was replaced by the Beauharnois Canal.

Today, the Soulanges Canal is a site that attracts kayakers and thousands of cyclists to the bike path that winds its way along its picturesque north side, providing all a little bit of quiet and a tiny glimpse of the past.

That historic past and the canal's recreational potential is what regional officials and members of the community are hoping to showcase as steps are being taken to preserve and promote its cultural significance.

"My hope is to attract as many people to the canal as possible," said Yvan Préfontaine, a Vaudreuil-Soulanges resident who has led the charge to arouse community interest in the canal. To promote the canal's development, he presented a model of 185 million-dollar project for the canal at the Saint Zotique library in 2018. The project was abandoned in 2003 due to lack of funding, but that hasn't stopped Préfontaine from seeing the canal's potential.

"The canal is largely surrounded by wooded areas and farmland, which sets it apart from the canals in Lachine and Sainte Anne de Bellevue," Préfontaine said. As the administrator of the Facebook group "Les amis du Canal de Soulanges," he features the canal's unique beauty and significance with photographs of its various landmarks, books about the canal's history, and advertising various events in the area.

In January, the Regional County Municipality of Vaudreuil-Soulanges announced a contest calling for submissions of plans to revitalize the canal site. Working with the Quebec Ministry of Culture and Communications, the initiative aims to develop a "vision rooted in the community and supported by the population," according to a statement issued by the MRC.

Described as a "revolutionary construction" project when it was built, boasting state-of-the art hydraulic and technological features, the MRC says the Soulanges Canal today is a "remarkable landscape entity" that it hopes to promote.

Under the supervision of Isabelle Giasson from EVOQ architectural firm, three finalists were selected from the over 800 submissions received.

An award-winning Montreal architect, Giasson received the Award of Excellence from the Canadian Society of Landscape Architects in 2021 for the documentary film series "Memoirs of Pioneering Landscape Architects of Quebec and Ontario." Her projects include the redevelopment of Leacock Street into a shared street on the downtown McGill University campus and drafting green infrastructure guidelines for the EXO public transportation system.

In April, three contest finalists were selected. They were chosen by an eight-member jury chaired by Philippe Lupien, a professor at the UQAM design school. Lupien is a landscape architect and host of the program Visite libre on Télé-Québec, a show that has been credited with popularizing architectural practices in residential homes throughout the province.

The three finalists, BC2 Groupe conseil inc., Fahey et associés in collaboration with Félix Dagenais, and Collectif Escargo in collaboration with Geneviève Murray will compete for the $1,000 prize. The RMC's website states that the winner will be announced this summer.

As future projects continue to take shape, remnants of the canal's former glory can still be spotted all around the area.

For example, located in Les Cèdres is the canal's original hydroelectric power plant, "Le Petit Pouvoir." Listed as a historic monument by the Quebec Ministry of Cultural Affairs in 1984, it is one of only four hydroelectric power plants built in Quebec before 1900. The power station was built in a castle-like architectural style that was popular at the time; the style can be seen in hotels like the Château Frontenac in Quebec City.

The generating station is located near the mid-way point of the canal. It provided the power for the canal's five motorized lock gates, allowed the wing bridges to operate and powered the lights along the canal.

Also, an old hotel at the entrance of the canal still stands, although the area is now used as camping grounds. These landmarks remain unused mementos of the past, although Pierre Lalonde, Mayor of Pointe des Cascades, is optimistic about the future of the canal as a patrimonial site.

"The four municipalities have signed an agreement and are working with the Ministry of Transportion to restore the area," said Lalonde, referring to the towns of Pointe des Cascades, Les Cèdres, Coteau du Lac and Les Coteaux. The mayor also noted that Pointe des Cascades is currently speaking with promoters to reopen the Théâtre des Cascades, which closed in 2019 after not being able to come to a lease agreement with the city.

In November 2020, the lighthouse in St. Pierre park in Pointe des Cascades was temporarily removed for restoration. Built in 1904, the 50-foot structure was in need of repair. After repairs which included patching up rust holes, reinforcing its stairs, and repainting the lighthouse in accordance with Ministry of Transportation standards, it was placed back in the park. It is one of four lighthouses along the canal.

Marianne Sigouin-Lebel, the Project Leader for the canal, says to expect more exciting new projects this summer. Her team will repair portions of the bike path, and will spotlight two "underused parks" by the canal's western mouth: Thomas Munro park in Coteau du Lac and Louis Stanislas Pariseau park in Les Coteaux.

Sigouin-Lebel said that the "ephemeral park" project will host various events along the canal, including concerts, microbrewery events, and free yoga. She also announced plans to pay tribute to the area's past by transforming Anchor Park in Pointe des Cascades into an open-air museum, with on-site guides to share historical information.

Between the regional plans to feature and preserve the history of the region and a growing community awareness, there is hope that the Soulanges canal will finally receive the attention it deserves.
