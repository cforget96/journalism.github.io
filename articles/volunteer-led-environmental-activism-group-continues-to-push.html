Volunteer-led environmental activism group continues to push 1019 region in right direction

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Sep 21, 2022

On April 28, the Communauté métropolitaine de Montréal (CMM) made a radical statement when they agreed to put a freeze on development on 12,367 hectares natural spaces. With this new protective law in place, a total of 53,435 hectares, or 22.3 per cent of the territory of Greater Montreal -- an area equivalent to the Island of Montreal -- are now subject to conservation measures.

While not all municipalities of Vaudreuil-Soulanges are part of the CMM, the municipalities that are --- Vaudreuil-Dorion, Saint Lazare, Hudson and the four municipalities on Ile Perrot -- make up the bulk of the population for the 1019 region. The protective law, known as a règlement de contrôle intérimaire (RCI), will have a major impact on the development of these cities moving forward.

For Mouvement MARE, a volunteer-led environmental activism organization in the region, the news is huge victory, though they won't take direct credit for it.

"The CMM's decision should be credited to the citizens who acted to protect natural spaces," said MARE spokesperson Judy-Fay Ferron. "We are not ecological experts. Our purpose is to support citizens and to provide them with the necessary information and guidance they might need."

Since 2020, MARE has worked with citizen groups like Nature Hudson and Pincourt Vert to organize and support a number of environmental preservation protests, including Sandy Beach in Hudson, Rousseau Forest in Pincourt and the Boisé of Vaudreuil-sur-le-Lac.

Since the beginning of 2022, the organization has also started meeting with municipal and regional leaders to encourage them to take concrete action to fight and resist climate change. So far, they have convinced nine different municipalities, including Saint-Lazare, Rigaud, and Coteau-du-Lac, to sign the "United for the Climate" declaration with the Union of Quebec Municipalities (UMQ).

Taking action from the inside

Last November, MARE member David Pharand was elected as a city councilor for the municipality of Sainte-Marthe-sur-le-Lac, where he has continued to spread climate change awareness.

"I think it was a good first year," Pharand said. "I was learning the ropes, but I think the community is moving in the right direction. Most importantly, we want to hear from our citizens to create a dialogue. We want to get them involved in ecological issues."

Pharand shared that some of the highlights in his first year as councilor were working with the Sainte-Marthe ecocitizen group, pledging to plant more than 400 new trees in the municipality, and signing the UMQ's climate declaration. Moving forward, he would like to work closely with the MRC on developing new ecological strategies.

Still more work ahead

While the CMM development freeze is a step in the right direction, many developers are fighting the decision. There have already been 11 legal contestations against the freeze. Canadian Senator and real estate developer Paul Massicotte requested that the RCI be declared illegal on May 18, a few weeks after the RCI was put in place. Two of his companies own land within the CMM.

Ferron says that more protests against the freeze should be expected.

"Those who disagree with the protection of natural environments will seek to exploit loopholes in the CMM's regulations," she said. "Citizens must remain on the lookout for dezoning and any other development changes, as well as participate in the public consultations that take place. We can't let our guard down."
