Lionel Groulx: A Vaudreuil native with a complicated legacy

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Aug 17, 2022

A Catholic priest. A historian. A writer. A Quebec nationalist. An anti-semite? Lionel Groulx was all these things at once, and his legacy in Quebec history is still the subject of debate and controversy 55 years after his death in 1967.

Groulx was born on January 13, 1878 at his childhood home 150 des Chenaux in Vaudreuil, which is still standing today. Built in the 1860s, the house is an example of the "boomtown" architectural style, which first appeared in new, rapidly developing American cities during the Western migration. With their flat roofs and efficient builds to maximize space, the houses are often associated with the working class.

The home, which remained in his family's possession until 1999, underwent major restorations when it was sold to new owners. The city of Vaudreuil-Dorion recognized it as a heritage site in 2005.

The son of a lumberjack, Groulx went to the village school before attending Sainte-Thérèse de Blainville seminary to become a Catholic priest, where he received the Governor-General's Medal in recognition of his academic excellence.

From 1900 to 1915, he taught literature and history at Valleyfield College, though he took a break from his teaching career between 1906 and 1909 to study theology and linguistics in Europe, earning a Ph.D. from the University of Rome.

Groulx left Valleyfield college in 1915 to become the first-ever chair of Canadian history at Université de Montreal, where he would remain active over the next three decades.

As a writer, Groulx first came to prominence in 1913 when he published an article for Le Devoir, arguing that Canadian history was not being given its due in universities and colleges in Quebec. Over his lifetime, he would publish 30 books of history and fiction, 20 shorter works, and hundreds of articles, some under pseudonyms.

In 1917, he founded L'Action Francaise, a monthly magazine run by priests and nationalists who advocated for the protection of the French language in Canada. The magazine occasionally flirted with the idea of political independence, but put the bulk of their efforts into advocating for the rights of the French minority within the Canadian confederation.

Informed by his Francophone-Catholic upbringing, much of his work romanticized the faith and virtue of old Quebec. In his eyes, all of French-Canadian history has been a struggle for survival against the continuing dominance of English Canada. At a time when the French language was in decline, particularly in the commercial and industrial worlds, his writings became increasingly influential.

The popularity of Groulx's writing made him a celebrity and a key Quebec nationalist figure at a time of economic strife: the Great Depression. He blamed the economic crisis on excessive industrialization in the province, which was backed by capitalist Americans. He believed an emphasis on agriculture would help make French Canadians self-sufficient.

Groulx had strong opinions about uplifting French Canadians, and often blamed other linguistic and ethnic groups for their problems. When the Second World War brought about a conscription crisis, Groulx openly accused anglophone Canadians of dividing the country on the issue, which was strongly opposed by the majority of French Canadians.

He also considered Jewish people a threat to the survival of French Canadians. In Action nationale, using the pseudonym Jacques Brassier, Groulx accused Jews of threatening the economy and spreading communist ideals. He also supported the "Achat chez nous" movement, which eventually supported the boycott of Jewish businesses in favour of French Canadian ones.

In 2020, a petition was filed to rename the Lionel-Groulx station after Montreal jazz legend Oscar Peterson. This triggered a collective debate about Groulx's historical legacy and the question of cultural representation of Black Canadians.

To many, Groulx is the spiritual father of Nationalist Québec, the creator of separatist myths. Much like the province he loved, Groulx's legacy is imbued with a deep passion which sometimes descended into exclusionism and hatred.

Quebecers will continue to debate the Vaudreuil native's place in the province's history, but no one can deny the indelible mark he left. He is buried in St-Michel cemetery in Vaudreuil.
