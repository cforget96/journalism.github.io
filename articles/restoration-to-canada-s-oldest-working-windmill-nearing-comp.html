Restoration to Canada's oldest working windmill nearing completion

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Oct 27, 2021

Built more than 300 years ago in 1708, the iconic landmark on Ile Perrot -- the historic stone windmill at Pointe-du-Moulin historic park in Notre Dame de l'Île Perrot -- will celebrate its restoration tomorrow with a special event. And the public is invited to attend.

The windmill is one of only two working windmills among the 18 still standing that date back to early French settlements in Quebec.

The latest restoration work, which began in 2019, will now allow for the actual milling of flour at the tower to be done again. Over the years, the function had been restored to the mill, but the wooden parts of the milling mechanism to replicate the original way the mill had been used faltered and necessitated new repairs. The last time flour was produced at the mill was in 2012.

"It's extremely exciting and long overdue," Charles-Olivier Bellerose-Bélanger, general manager of the park, said last week, referring to the reopening of the mill's operations.

The windmill had been in need of repair and in 2016, patrons and staff of the park were devastated when the sails of the windmill finally broke off.

"Water often gets trapped in the axis, and this can cause the wood to rot. It was a really unpleasant surprise," explains Bellerose-Bélanger.

The incident put pressure on the provincial government to fix the windmill. After two years of restoration work, the landmark should be operational within the next few weeks.

The Quebec government took over management of the site over from the federal government in 1973. After completing restorations, the park, which includes the original miller's house complete with a wood-burning oven, was reopened in 1979. That summer, about 15,000 tourists visited the park.

"It was a very popular attraction at the time," Bellerose-Bélanger said. "It would have been similar to Upper Canada Village."

The park has welcomed visitors ever since. In addition to being able to tour the grounds surround the mill, visitors can take in the view of the island of Montreal and learn about the history of the area.

In fact, one item of interest that they learn is how one of the main streets on Île Perrot came to be named after the fictional literary character Don Quichotte. The fabled hero of Miguel De Cervantes's novel that dates back to the early 1600s famously attempted to attack a windmill, mistaking it for a giant.

The event at the Pointe-du-Moulin historic park tomorrow is a fundraising event that will benefit the non-profit group that operates the park and manages staff. It will

feature two theatrical performances from Chêne-Bleu secondary school students and a virtual auction. It begins at 5 p.m.
