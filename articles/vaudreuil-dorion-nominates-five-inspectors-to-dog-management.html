Vaudreuil-Dorion nominates five inspectors to dog management task force

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Dec 8, 2021

A group of five inspectors in Vaudreuil-Dorion now have the powers of inspection, seizure and investigation to ensure that dog owners are following the city's bylaws. The move comes as a part of an Act that manages dog activity to promote human safety.

The team is comprised of inspectors from three different entities: Christophe Allitt, the coordinator for the Vaudreuil-Dorion municipal patrol; Catherine Leclerc and Félix Villeneuve, two members of the municipal patrol employed by Neptune Security; and Chantal Vermette and Richard Seymour from Animal Control Vaudreuil-Soulanges.

In June 2021, the town passed a bylaw that required all dogs to be microchipped for the purpose of tracking and identifying lost or potentially dangerous dogs. Failure to comply with the bylaw could result in a fine of $1,000 upon a first offense.

The push to surveil dog ownership comes on the heels of the provincial government's new dangerous dog law, which came into effect in March 2021. According to the new provincial rules, which are aimed at preventing dog attacks, municipalities are required to enforce the provincial law that allows a dog deemed to be dangerous to be euthanized. The provincial law also allows for towns to adopt their own restrictions on dog ownership.

Town officials say that the additional bylaws are in place so the city has an overview of canine activity in the area. The city also reserves the right to photograph every dog for identification purposes.

"It is not known how many residents of Vaudreuil-Dorion own dogs, but we certainly have more dogs than registered," explained mayor Guy Pilon. Pilon added that the new bylaw will help the town keep track more efficiently and aid in reuniting lost animals with their owners.

Over the past few months, the city has been conducting an information campaign about the changes to municipal and provincial laws. Patrol officers regularly conduct preventive checks with citizens on the street or in dog parks.

While the city anticipates some dog owners to be uncomfortable with the additional surveillance of dog ownership, they want to remind residents that the bylaws are put in place to protect, not to control.

"Once citizens become aware that these laws are in place to protect them, collaboration will become natural and each citizen in turn becomes a link in our chain of communication," said Allitt.

As laws around dog ownership tighten, the town is also becoming more dog-friendly; the city announced that the newly-acquired Harwood nature park with feature a nearly two-acre dog park for residents to enjoy with their canine counterparts.
