Île aux Tourtes archaeological dig yields remains of Confederation-era home

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Jan 19, 2022

An archeological survey of an island under the Île aux Tourtes Bridge has turned up ruins of a residence dating back to the 19th century that is believed to have been the home of a prominent British doctor who became a pioneer in medical education and radiography in Canada.

The finds, which date back to the time of Canadian Confederation, were discovered on Girdwood Island, a small uninhabited island just off the shores of Senneville the lies directly under the Île aux Tourtes Bridge. The survey of the island was commissioned by the Quebec Transport Ministry and conducted in the summer of 2021 in preparation for construction of the new Île aux Tourtes Bridge scheduled to begin some time after 2023.

This island, which is part of the municipality of Senneville, gets its name from Dr. Gilbert Prout Girdwood and his wife, Fanny Merriman Blackwell Girdwood, who inhabited the island in the late 19th and early 20th century.

Born in England, Girdwood came to Canada as a member of the British army during a diplomatic dispute with the United States at the time of the American Civil War. Girdwood decided to leave the British army to live in Canada permanently in 1864. Graduating from McGill University the following year, he became a prominent medical expert in Montreal, specializing in the field of radiography, working as a professor at McGill and medical staff officer for the Canadian militia.

"The project uncovered the remains of the Girdwood residence, as well as the remains of greenhouses that were used to grow vines," said Transport Quebec spokesperson Sarah Bensadoun. "The interventions allowed us to document the construction of the buildings and the operation of the greenhouses. Additionally, the excavation of the dump on Girdwood Island led to the discovery of numerous domestic artifacts from the 19th and 20th centuries."

The artifacts will be catalogued and kept by the Laboratoire et la Réserve d 'archéologie du Québec, which is administered by the Quebec Culture and Communications Ministry, Bensadoun said.

While much of the Girdwoods' lives took place in Montreal, the couple would escape city life to spend time at their home on the island just off the shores of Senneville. Several archival photographs from the McCord Museum dating back to 1866, believed to be taken by Girdwood show his wife at the ruins of Fort Senneville, which was burned to the ground during a battle with the Iroquois in 1691.

The surveys of the island commissioned by the provincial transport ministry were carried out by Artefactuel, private company specializing in the archeological heritage of Quebec. The $100,000 project included underwater excavation, sonar coverage and archaeological dives, adding to previous archaeological projects carried out in the area in 2014 and 2019.

Bensadoun said there are no plans for any further study at this time, but added that if any

geological, geotechnical, historical or archaeological items of interest or value are discovered during the construction of the new bridge, the project could resume.

Although there is no firm date as to when construction of the new two-kilometre Île aux Tourtes span is set to begin, completion is expected by 2028 or 2029.
