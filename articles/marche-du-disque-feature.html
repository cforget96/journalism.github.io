Marche du Disque feature

By Christopher Forget · Feb 16, 2022

Breathing life into the Faubourg

As many long-time tenants of the Faubourg de l'Ile are being forced to close their doors, Marché du Disque is trying to rebuild a sense of community---and it's only getting started

CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report

There once was a time when the parking lot at the Faubourg de l'Ile in Pincourt was jam-packed on weekends. If you walk through the Faubourg on a Saturday in 2022, however, you might be taken aback by the number of vacant commercial spaces. But it isn't all bad---if you look, and listen, a little closer, you might find that the future of the mall is not as bleak as it first appears.

Enter Marché du Disque, owned and run by Alex Pantis, a 25-year-old biology student, and his grandfather, Denis. Although the franchise only moved into the Faubourg in 2014, the franchise is one of the oldest surviving record stores in Montreal, having opened its flagship location on the corner of Berri and Ste. Catherine in 1984.

A legend of Quebec music, the elder Pantis is often credited with importing rock-and-roll to the province. Working as a singer and producer of "yé-yé" music in the 1960s, his life was the subject of the 2020 documentary titled Jukebox: an American Dream in Quebec.

With Denis as his grandfather, it's no wonder Alex Pantis is passionate about music and collecting records, calling himself "my own best customer."

Pantis began working at the Mount Royal location of Marche du Disque when he was in eighth grade. After that location closed, Pantis decided to open a location at the Faubourg de l'Ile in his first year of CEGEP, citing the affordable rent and the mall's proximity to John Abbott College.

Since arriving at the mall, Pantis has moved locations---and expanded---twice. And the store seems to be doing better than ever.

"It's gotten to the point where I need to start staffing more than two people on Saturdays to handle the amount of clients we get. I never thought it would get to this point," Pantis said.

"We're starting to acquire more and more rare records, which has helped us build a niche clientele. We sometimes have customers drive over three hours to check out the store," said store manager Daniel Racette, 20.

Marche du Disque is certainly a specialty store, but its variety of vinyl records, books, instruments and memorabilia makes it inviting for casual mallgoers to pop in for a quick look around, especially when the pandemic has made it difficult for other stores to keep their doors open. During these trying times, the shop has become a haven for many regulars.

"After the first wave of COVID, a customer thanked me and told me how much the store meant to him. That was the biggest compliment for me," Pantis recalled.

While Pantis is undoubtedly satisfied with the store's success, he still feels like there is more to accomplish and wants to continue building in Pincourt.

"I want to build a destination, a place where anyone can find something they like, whether they're 16 or 60. Eventually, I'd like to host different kinds of shows and become a bigger presence in the community," he said.

We're waist-deep in the Amazon Age, and as old ways of business become less tenable, something may be lost---a sense of community and local culture. As the mall continues to search for new tenants, Pantis and his staff are examples of how local businesses make towns like Pincourt a better place to live.
