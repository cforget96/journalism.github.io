What's with all the ladybugs?

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Undated

Whether you love them for their cute little shells or hate them because they're turning up in and around your home, it's been hard to ignore the sudden abundance of ladybugs in the past month.

Actually, these orange-shelled critters are not ladybugs at all; they're called Asian lady beetles. This may seem like a minor distinction, but in fact, these beetles should be regarded very differently than their red-shelled cousins.

"First introduced in the 1980's in the southern USA to control aphids, it has spread north and is now found throughout most of southern Canada. It loves to eat aphids (a common plant pest); so as the aphid population increases during warm weather, so does the population of the Asian lady beetle," explained David Wees, Associate director of the Farm Management and Technology program at McGill University's Macdonald Campus.

The heat wave has brought on more of these beetles, and if you find them bothersome, you'd have good reason. Unlike the red ladybug, which is essentially harmless, the Asian lady beetle can bite, is harmful to dogs, and likes to invade homes. They also leave behind an foul odor and a trace of yellow fluid, which is another trait that distinguishes it from the red ladybug.

"There are now so many Asian lady beetles present in our area that they are crowding out the native species of ladybugs," added Wees.

In most cultures, ladybugs are considered a sign of good luck. Unfortunately, the overabundance of the beetle this year may be too much of a good thing. As the fall weather begins to set in, be prepared: you might find a few more of these orange-shelled guests in your home than usual.
