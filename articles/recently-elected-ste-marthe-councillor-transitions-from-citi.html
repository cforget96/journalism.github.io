Recently-elected Ste-Marthe councillor transitions from citizen activist to municipal official

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Nov 24, 2021

David Pharand is no stranger to leadership. Since the inception of the M.A.R.E. group (Mouvement d'Action Régional en Environment) over two years ago, he has been a prominent member of the citizen-led environmental activism collective that works closely with municipal and regional governments. Now, he finds himself on the other side of the equation; on Nov. 7, Pharand was elected as city councillor for the town of Ste-Marthe.

Although the town is small in terms of population with just over 1000 residents, at 79.8 km2, it is the third-largest municipality in the region by surface area, trailing only Rigaud (99.1 km2) and Ste-Justine-de-Newton (84.6 km2). For this reason, Pharand sees the town as key part of environmental conservation in the Vaudreuil-Soulanges region.

"The municipality's wooded areas allow a connection between the forests of St-Lazare, Mont-Rigaud and Sainte-Justine-de-Newton. Fortunately, in the past decade, two landowners have sold their territories to the municipality and are now under conservation. It is our duty to ensure a better protection of these natural environments," Pharand told The 1019 Report.

As a member of the M.A.R.E. group, Pharand helped submit three proposals to the Vaudreuil-Soulanges RCM (regional county municipality) last July: To assess the natural environment of the region in order to establish an effective conservation plan, to establish a conservation target of 17% of the territory, and to establish a reforestation plan to increase forest cover to nearly 50%. Now that he is a municipal government elect, he has the ability to effect change on a bureaucratic level. But this comes with its challenges; at present, Ste-Marthe does not meet these targets.

Pharand has already laid the groundwork for his mission to improve the environmental future of the town. His first mandate as councillor is to plant over 420 trees, one for each vote received in the municipal election, by the end of 2022.

But his mission needs to go beyond conservation. Because so much of the municipality is agricultural land, Pharand understands that sustainable agricultural practices are vital to the town's future. His transition from engaged citizen to municipal official will begin by improving communication between the people and their government.

"Although I'm still involved with M.A.R.E., the work I do as a councillor will be different from my work as a citizen. My priority will be to create a dialogue with the citizens of Ste-Marthe about the environment. From what I see so far, it seems like these concerns are already very close to the hearts of the residents," said Pharand.
