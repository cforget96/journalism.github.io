Letter to Marie-Claude Nichols

By Christopher Forget · Undated

Dear Madame Nichols,

I wish I could introduce myself under happier circumstances, but I am reaching out to you as a 26-year-old journalist for Brenda O'Farrell's newspaper The 1019 Report and an English professor at Marianopolis College to express my opposition to Bill 96, with the hope that my perspective will be heard and represented.

I must preface this letter by making clear that I am a proud Quebecer. The Montreal Canadiens are as sacred to me as they are to any of my francophone friends and family. I grew up speaking French with friends on my hockey team and with my cousins. When I was a student, I worked as a service manager at IGA and at Tommy Hilfiger, where I spoke French at a high level every day. Even though I work for an anglophone newspaper, I communicate with municipal officials exclusively in French on a weekly basis. Quebec is the only home I have ever known, and French has always been a part of it.

I will try to make this as brief as possible, but my fear is that it will be very difficult for me to continue the career I have been working towards for eight years, and I fear it will be impossible for the young teens who will be affected by this Bill to follow in my footsteps. The ministry of education projects there to be a 23 per cent increase in student population in 2029, and this large cohort of young students will have their right to a higher English education robbed from them.

My CEGEP years exposed me to ideas I had never encountered before. The beauty of CEGEP is that students are free to choose what they learn at exactly the time they are filled with a blend of curiosity and optimism that is unique to those on the cusp of adulthood.

In my case, I became interested in journalism when I attended John Abbott College between 2013 and 2015. I took a class on writing for broadcast media with another fellow journalist and college teacher, Tracey McKee. I enjoyed her assignments, which involved boiling down sentences until only the essential information remained. Her class made exposed me to the possibility of writing as a career.

It was also during this time that I decided to become a CEGEP English teacher. I went on to study English literature in university from 2015 to 2020, receiving my BA and MA from McGill. Now, I've made it to the other side, as both a journalist and a teacher. I'm proud and thankful to be doing work I'm passionate about, and Bill 96 threatens to permanently end this dream career I have worked so hard to create.

Bill 96 threatens to decimate CEGEP English departments in particular. Over the past few weeks, the Director Generals at Dawson and Vanier, the two largest English colleges in Quebec, have speculated that their English departments could be cut by 50 per cent, which would essentially make it impossible to continue my career as a CEGEP teacher.

This statement is both alarming and frustrating, not only because I have spent a good part of my life working to get here, but it also means that Quebec youth, whether they are classified as francophone, anglophone, or allophone, will not have the same opportunities to study English at the highest level as I did. Attending an English CEGEP would no longer offer students the opportunity to master English.

This change would set a dangerous precedent in the province, one that could affect local anglophone media. Many anglophone journalists come through the CEGEP and Concordia journalism system. If CEGEPs cannot offer high-level English education, it will not prepare them for English university, which may push students to study elsewhere. Students may not have the luxury of learning from experienced journalists like Brenda O'Farrell or Tracey McKee. Without proper English education, how will anglophones be able to properly express themselves and represent themselves in media?

Although I have personal stake in opposing this Bill, I am most concerned for the youth. Before I was a CEGEP teacher, I worked as a private tutor in English and French. In 2019, I began to teach a 13-year-old girl who had recently immigrated from China, and I continue to meet with her regularly to this day. I principally tutor her in French and Quebec history. She has made great strides in her French, but the pandemic has made her road to fluency especially tumultuous. When high schools were forced to offer asynchronous classes, she had very little time to practice French with anyone but me. In the winter of 2021, her parents made the decision to travel back to China to be with her grandparents. She spent that semester at an international school outside of Shanghai, but we continued to meet to practice French despite the 12-hour time difference. She was back in-person at a Quebec high school for the 2021-2022 school year, but the past two years have set her back, even though her parents have done their best to make sure she can keep up with her peers.

Being a teenage immigrant is hard enough, but the pandemic presented a new set of challenges for her to overcome. She has made it this far, and regardless of the outcome of Bill 96, I know she will grow up to be a successful adult in this province. My concern, though, is that the choices she has will be severely limited compared to the choices I had growing up. These limitations are simplify unacceptable in a country like Canada, in a province like Quebec.

Sincerely, Christopher Forget
