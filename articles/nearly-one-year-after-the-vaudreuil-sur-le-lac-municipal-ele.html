Nearly one year after the Vaudreuil-sur-le-Lac municipal election, mayor Philip Lapalme is delivering on his promise to preserve the du Boisé woods

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Oct 13, 2021

In December 2020, the tiny municipality of Vaudreuil sur le Lac held a by-election. There was one issue being debated at that time -- saving a large wooded area from development.

For the small village of just under 1,400 residents located behind the Costco in Vaudreuil-Dorion, losing the woods meant losing its quiet, countryside identity.

"The woods insulates our community; it is also a green lung and defends us from noise," explained Marie-Agnès Huberlant, a prominent activist in the citizen-led Regroupement pour la protection du Boisé du VSLL.

The group wanted to save the nearly 20-hectare wooded area from becoming the site of a xxxxx-unit housing development, which had been proposed by a development firm that owns the land.

The by-election was scheduled because during the pandemic, a number of members of the municipal council -- including former mayor Claude Pilon -- quit, citing health reasons.

The municipality's current mayor, Philip Lapalme, built his campaign around the protection of the woods. On Oct. 1, as the nomination deadlines for the planned Nov. 7 municipal election, Lapalme was acclaimed to a new full term in office.

In the months following his election in December 2020, the citizen group was disappointed by the municipal government's inaction in relation to saving the du Boisé woods.

"The municipality told us they would put together a consultation committee, but the committee never met. At the time, it gave us the impression that we didn't have a partner in our cause," said Huberlant.

The land is owned by two private entities: the Farand group and the Baer group. Only one group, Farand, has the authorization to develop. The Baer family sold the majority of the forest to Farand, who developed some of the land in the mid-2000s. Farand has expressed interest in developing more of the land, but the city proposed a temporary protection bylaw in 2017 that required Farand to receive authorization from the Ministry of Environment for each new project. The citizens' protection group pointed out that if the land owners received the authorization to build, the municipal government would have to move quickly to save it from being cut down.

The citizen group enlisted the help of Mouvement M.A.R.E., a group that supports environmental activists across the Vaudreuil-Soulanges region, to improve communication with the municipal government. By the summer of 2021, the city had conversations with the owners about acquiring the woods.

"It's a long process, and last year we were worried the city wasn't acting fast enough. Now, we're encouraged by their efforts," said Huberlant.

For mayor Philip Lapalme, the next step is to reduce, as much as possible, the amount that each citizen would have to pay in annual taxes, should the municipal government acquire the woods. The village is eligible for funding from La Trame verte et bleue, an initiative from the Montreal Metropolitan Area to promote the recreational and touristic use of natural environment. This funding would reduce the cost, but it has yet to be determined how much each resident would have to pay.

"Both ownership groups have expressed a willingness to sell. We conducted two studies: a valuation of the land and the valuation of the land in the current market. We still need to determine a purchase cost, which must be arranged between us and the owners, who are also conducting valuations" said Lapalme.

Eventually, he would like to develop the park to make it more accessible to citizens. Currently, residents who live closest to the park have been maintaining the woods.

Lapalme is aware how much the woods means to the community, and intends to keep the promise he made when he was elected.

"Here in Vaudreuil-sur-le-Lac, I know we value tranquility. In my nine months as mayor, I think my administration and I have made a lot of progress to maintain that, considering how big a case this is. I'm quite optimistic that we can have this settled in the next few months."
