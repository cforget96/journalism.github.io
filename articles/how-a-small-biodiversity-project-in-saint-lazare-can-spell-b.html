How a small biodiversity project in Saint Lazare can spell big change for the region

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Dec 8, 2021

So, what should we do when we become numb to the statistics about climate change? It may require a fundamental reevaluation of how we view our way of life, and ourselves, in relation to nature.

This heavy ecological and social issue might seem too big and too broad for Vaudreuil-Soulanges, but, in fact, the region is a perfect example of an area where leaders must take action. Between 2016 and 2019, Vaudreuil-Soulanges experienced an average annual growth rate of 17.3, the fifth-highest rate of any RCM in Quebec; this fact is even more significant because the region is already one of the ten largest RCMs in the province, including Montreal and Quebec City. The COVID-19 pandemic has contributed to the rise in population in the area, with many people moving off-island for lower cost of living and more space. This shift has created tension in many of the region's municipalities, as the push to conserve forested areas was a significant factor in the recent elections in Pincourt and Vaudreuil sur le Lac.

In many ways, municipal governments have become the front line for the struggle against the biodiversity crisis, and citizens are demanding their leaders educate themselves and adapt to an evolving balance between development and conservation.

One project in Saint Lazare exemplifies a municipal government rising to the occasion. Over the past few years, the city has reviewed its development and management practices for public spaces in an effort to reduce negative impacts on the environment and take concrete action to promote and preserve biodiversity. The study has now yielded tangible results in the community.

"Across the territory, many spaces are taking on a more natural look, using plant diversity that allows traditional grass and mowing to be replaced by a more ecological approach to landscaping management. Plants that are better suited to the soil have been permanently added to public spaces, allowing residents to discover alternatives to water- and energy-hungry grass areas," explained a statement from city officials.

Unlike the grass typically used by residents and municipalities, the plant life the city is using is indigenous to the area. As a result, these green spaces will require less maintenance, which allows the city to reduce its water consumption.

"It is the city's responsibility to manage green spaces such as traffic circles, street rights-of-way, and parks. Traditionally, grass is used to 'fill in' vacant spaces, but it is poorly adapted to soil conditions and our climate. The native plants require a period of adaptation and time to allow them to reach their full potential, but eventually they will become more resistant," said Lyne Mousseau, Project Manager for the Environment Department of Saint Lazare.

The project, which is a product of years of planning and studying, will undoubtedly have a positive impact on biodiversity in the region. But science-based projects alone will not be enough to turn the ecological tide. Real change requires a change in mindset, too.

"As a society, we imagine the human world and the natural world as two separate spaces. The natural spaces we find in municipalities reflects a specific idea we have of nature that is compatible with human life. Take gardens, for example. These are often considered to be green spaces, but in reality, they are highly artificial natural spaces. Truly 'wild' life, on the other hand, signifies that we're not supposed to be there," explained Louis-Etienne Pigeon, a professor of environmental ethics at L'Université de Laval in Quebec City. "This is a cultural problem we need to solve so we can begin to reverse the damage of climate change."

Mousseau recognizes that a shift in thinking needs to occur, and hopes that this project will help Saint-Lazare residents to adapt their idea of natural beauty.

"It is essential to review our cultural practices in order to reduce our impact on the environment. For decades, green grass has been a symbol of success and beauty. Some people find [this project ] to be less beautiful because they still believe in this outdated concept. This is a dramatic change. The more new eco-friendly landscaping becomes synonymous with fertile soil and a healthier living environment, the more it will become the norm. You have to see a flowering meadow in bloom, it 's beautiful, colourful and so alive!" Mousseau said.

"One of my neighbours, who is a biologist, began going into the neighbourhood forest to incorporate native plants on his property. Over a number of years, the plants have grown and adapted, and the results are stunning. The problem, though, is not everyone is a biologist! This type of planning needs to be included the budget so that everyone who wants to make a change can take part. Saint-Lazare seems to be doing a great job, but I think the provincial government should develop a program that could help small municipalities with biodiversity," Pigeon said.

While the biodiversity project may take about three years to be fully realized, city officials believe it is important to lead by example so that citizens will follow suit.

"I believe everyone has the social responsibility to care about each other and our future. As a municipality, not only do we have to ensure the well-being of our citizens, but we also have to be a role model in our actions. That's why the town has been taking a more ecological approach to landscaping management," said Saint Lazare mayor Geneviève Lachance.

Saint Lazare becomes a monarch-friendly town

Keeping in line with the town's imperative to become more biodiverse, Saint Lazare mayor Geneviève Lachance has joined the leaders of over 300 communities across North America by taking the Mayors' Monarch Pledge.

The pledge, which was created by the U.S. National Wildlife Federation and promoted in Canada by the David Suzuki Foundation, is a commitment to create monarch habitat and engage their residents in educational and conservation projects. There is no grant attached to the pledge.

The monarch population has dropped by 90 per cent in the last two decades, so the pledge is a gesture from municipalities to help bring the butterflies back from the brink of extinction. Municipal governments are uniquely positioned to tangibly improve conditions for monarchs.

"Being a proximity government allows us to be closer to our residents and have a more direct impact, as well has enabling their active involvement in different projects. The monarch-friendly community idea came from a resident who had reached out to me as a result of her involvement with the David Suzuki Foundation Butterflyway Project. It would be really difficult to have the same direct impact and see such quick results at the provincial or federal level," said Lachance.

The city had already taken the initiative to plant and protect milkweed and to organize educational campaigns in the community about protecting monarchs. Saint Lazare joins Vaudreuil Dorion, Coteau du Lac and Les Coteaux as the only official monarch-friendly towns in the 1019 region.

Recommended native plants:

"In general, 'noble' native species are preferred such as maples, oaks, walnut trees, white pines, yellow birches (not white or gray), and serviceberry trees," said Lyne Mousseau, Project Manager for the Environment Department of Saint Lazare. A list of native perennial flowers and shrubs are available at espacepourlavie.ca/en/native-plants-your-garden.
