Equi LibreFINAL

By Christopher Forget · Aug 17, 2022

Where horses and helping come together

Therapeutic riding centre in St. Lazare on its way to becoming top Canadian training facility

CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report

At first, Down Syndrome and horseback riding might seem like an unlikely pair. But for Marie-Josée Bélanger, they make perfect sense together.

Born with Down Syndrome, a genetic disorder that causes lifelong intellectual disability and developmental delays, Bélanger's parents signed her up for therapeutic horse-riding sessions when she was two years old. It marked the beginning of a special connection she developed with horses.

"Riding has allowed her to build strength and confidence," said Bélanger's mother Terrie Bélanger-Ardron. "Over the years, riding has been the only therapy she's stuck with, and I think it is because of her connection with animals, horses in particular."

Now 29, Bélanger continues to benefit from therapeutic riding. While she is riding, she is able to multi-task, listen to instructions, and keep her posture, things her mother says she struggles to do other settings.

"Most of all, she smiles," Bélanger-Ardron said.

Today, Bélanger rides at Équi-Libre Therapeutic Riding Centre in St. Lazare, and was one of the first clients at the centre when co-founder Devon O'Farrell opened the facility in 2016.

In 2018, after a stroke in left Bélanger partially paralyzed, Équi-Libre employed a special technique that helped her rebuild strength and ride again. Despite her fragile health, she continued riding over the pandemic because, being outdoors, it was the safest form of therapy.

"It was very helpful for her," Bélanger-Ardron said. "It helped me, too."

Bélanger is an example of the life-changing work that goes on at Équi-Libre. O'Farrell stressed that, above all, Équi-Libre is a place where anyone, regardless of ability, financial situation or age can benefit from the services they offer.

Équi-Libre was initially created out of O'Farrell's two passions: horses and helping other people.

"My parents were always involved in volunteering, so from a young age, I was used to working with people with differences," O'Farrell said. "I studied Human Relations at Concordia and initially wanted to become a social worker. Then, I discovered therapeutic horse riding when I interned at a centre in Ormstown, and I knew it was what I wanted to do. I had been riding since I was eight, and had always loved horses. I think the fact my father started his own successful business made the process of starting my own therapeutic centre less daunting."

O'Farrell began with two horses [renting an arena by the hour]{.mark}. Six years later, the centre has [28 acres, 22 horses, 8 large paddocks]{.mark} and serves more than 80 clients a week. It has also recently registered as a non-profit organization, an achievement O'Farrell is particularly proud of.

"Becoming a not-for-profit has always been our goal," O'Farrell said. "And our growth has made that possible. As the only therapeutic riding centre in the area, there is almost always a waiting list here."

Équi-Libre now has a staff of about 15, which includes instructors, coaches, administration and barn staff. The centre also relies on a large roster of volunteers to help the operation run smoothly. The work is demanding and employees and volunteers often have several responsibilities.

"I wear quite a few hats at Équi-Libre," said head instructor Kendra Flynn-Stronach. "I'm responsible for training and mentoring our program instructors as well as overseeing program safety standards and content. I'm also Équi-Libre's bookkeeper and a bit of a maintenance person. I love figuring out how to build and fix things, and something is always in need of repair or maintenance on an equestrian property."

Flynn-Stronach joined Équi-Libre in 2018 after working at a therapeutic riding centre in Ashburn, Ont. While working there, she gained experience as a riding coach at the World Equestrian Games and Paralympics. She says horses drew her to the profession initially, but the effect working with horses has on clients -- and on the staff -- is what stirs her passion.

"The sense of freedom, independence and partnership that our riders benefit from is a contagious feeling, and it impacts everyone around them," she said.

The centre offers a number of services, including therapeutic riding, equine-assisted learning through horsemanship, a competitive riding program, occupational therapy for kids, riding lessons and a summer camp. The centre was initially called Enfants en Équi-Libre, but O'Farrell decided to change the name when her clientele expanded. "We started out small, with therapeutic riding and equine-assisted learning, the two most popular programs in Canada, but we're trying to address needs within the community. We recently started a program for veterans and have a few other programs in the pipeline," O'Farrell said.

While most therapeutic horse-riding centres mainly serve children, Équi-Libre sees the value in the relationship between horses and [all kinds of people.]{.mark}

"Horses are exceptional in the way they communicate non-verbally," O'Farrell said. "Their size can be impressive, and you get a feeling of self-confidence when you accomplish or have them do something. We really see the way it impacts our clients."

Because the horses are constantly adapting to different riders with different needs and temperaments, the work can be mentally demanding. There are currently 11 horses working in the program, and all of them have to go through a 30-day trial period before working with clients.

"Horses can be skittish and we need to make sure they have a good temperament for the job," O'Farrell explained. "For example, if a child with autism is riding, they might distribute their weight differently or make a noise that could make the horse anxious. We have to make sure the horses are properly trained and adapted for those situations."

Despite the centre's success in a short period of time, O'Farrell wants to build on the momentum she and her staff have created. The next step is for Équi-Libre to become only the second fully credited Canadian Therapeutic Riding Association exam centre, where candidates train to become therapeutic instructors. She also wants to work towards Canadian charitable status.

"We want to make our centre as accessible as possible, and becoming a charity. And ramping up fundraising will help us make the service available to anyone," O'Farrell said. "Accessibility and inclusivity have always been some of our core goals."
