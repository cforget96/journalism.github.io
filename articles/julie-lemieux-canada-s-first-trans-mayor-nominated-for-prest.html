Julie Lemieux, Canada's first trans mayor, nominated for prestigious municipal award

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Sep 21, 2022

After becoming the first trans mayor in Canada when she was elected in 2017 by the people of Très-Saint-Rédempteur, a small municipality on the Quebec-Ontario border, Julie Lemieux has continued to be an example of integrity, confidence, and leadership in her community. That's why she has been named a finalist for the Jean-Marie-Moreau award, a distinction administered by the Fédération québécoise des municipalités (FQM).

"I am very proud of this nomination from the FQM, which demonstrates a real desire for openness and inclusion in the municipal world," Lemieux said. "It has always been important to me to be there for people. I will continue to get involved, because there is still a lot of work to be done for the rights of the LGBTQ2+ community, to break its isolation and promote its development."

The award, which recognizes the exceptional commitment, leadership, and dynamism of an elected municipal official in their community, was created in honor of Jean-Marie Moreau, the president of l'Union des conseils de comté du Québec (UCCQ), now known as the FQM, from 1970 to 1983.

When Lemieux was re-elected in 2021, she became the first-ever trans mayor to do so---the first in the world. It is not a distinction she treats lightly, and she has taken it upon herself to represent all minorities in her work on the municipal level and with the Vaudreuil-Soulanges MRC.

In her first year after being re-elected in Très-Saint-Rédempteur, a small town of 863 residents, Lemieux has worked on creating new green spaces, including a community garden. Lemieux and her team also made improvements to the town library, which is the oldest active library in Vaudreuil-Soulanges and was inaugurated by former Quebec Premier Daniel Johnson when he was serving as MNA of Vaudreuil-Soulanges in 1988.

On the regional level, Lemieux serves on seven different committees for the Vaudreuil-Soulanges MRC, including the public security, social development, and cultural committees. She also helped create LGBTQ2+ Vaudreuil-Soulanges, a new project that aims to build an inclusive society through advocacy and education and to support vulnerable communities by giving them spaces to express themselves freely.

"The region is growing and is becoming more diverse, and this organization will be a resource for people of all genders, orientations, ethnicities, or languags," Lemieux said. "It's important to have this kind of support system in Vaudreuil-Soulanges and for the population to support these minority groups, as well."

2021 was a year with many ups, but not without its share of downs for Lemieux. On July 8, the mayor suffered a stroke and was forced to take leave from her municipal work. She's recently taken up her responsibilities again and, thankfully, has made a healthy recovery.

As for the award nomination, Lemieux is being cautiously optimistic.

"It's bittersweet, in a way. I would be grateful for the honor, but I also don't want to be disappointed if I don't get it," she joked. "In any case, I feel like the nomination is not only for the work I've done. It represents a big step for all trans people in Quebec, and whatever I 've accomplished, it's been with a team as passionate as I am. In life, you never accomplish anything alone."

The winner of the Jean-Marie-Moreau award will be announced at the 80th Congress of the Quebec Federation of Municipalities on Friday, September 23 at the Palais des Congrès in Montreal, where Lemieux will be operating a kiosk for LBGTQ2+ awareness in municipalities. She will also be part of a panel on how municipalities can support minority communities.
