Restoration plans for Pointe-Claire windmill take shape

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Undated

Ever since the Pointe-Claire Heritage Preservation Society was formed in 2000, the group has pushed to restore its iconic 312-year-old windmill located along the Pointe-Claire village shore.

"When Pointe-Claire was incorporated as a town in 1910, the windmill was its symbol, and it still is," said Andrew Swidzinski, President of the Heritage Preservation Society. "The town even gets its name from the point where the windmill is located, so we feel that it is important that it be restored to its original form and that it be made open to the public."

The windmill is in a state of deterioration. It was only when two wings fell off the windmill after a major windstorm in 2019 that the current owners, the Archdiocese of Montreal, took more direct interest in restoring the heritage building.

The project will restore the windmill to working order, meaning that it will be able to mill flour the same way it did 300 years ago. The full restoration serves a dual purpose: it would add an attraction for tourism, but, perhaps more importantly, the rotation of the turbine will prevent mildew buildups, which contributed to the windmill's deterioration in the first place.

The total cost for the restoration project will be $1.9 million and would also require a large contingency reserve to account for possible inflation. The plans began to come together once the city of Pointe-Claire came to an agreement to provide $967,761, or half the total amount, as subsidy for the Archdiocese. The agreement also guarantees public access to the windmill once it is restored.

"The Archdiocese is now turning to private funding and to the Quebec Ministry of Culture," Swidzinski said. "They are currently in the application process with the Ministry, but all the Archdiocese was missing for them to treat the demand was for the city to become a financial backer. Now that that's done, the Ministry should make the decision in the next few weeks."

Built in 1710 to promote settlement in the area, the Sulpician Society of Priests commissioned Léonard Paillard for the job, a millwright who had already built or repaired more than 14 windmills, including the Senneville, Pointe-aux-Trembles, Rivière-des-Prairies and Ile-Perrot mills. Because European settlements encroached on Iroquois land at the time, the windmill served as a refuge in case there were raids, as evinced by the "murder holes" that people could stick their muskets out of.

The windmill was fully operational until 1866 and underwent major alterations in the decades that followed. In 1895, the windmill's dilapidated roof was removed and replaced by an observation platform, on which a wind engine was installed to pump water to the neighbouring convent.

The Pointe-Claire windmill was classified as a heritage building in 1983 by the Quebec Ministry of Culture. In Swidzinski's view, it deserves to be treated as such.

"The windmill is one of the two oldest buildings in Pointe-Claire, and one of the oldest in the Montreal area. There are only about a dozen remaining windmills from the New France area, so it will be nice to bring this one back to life," he said.
