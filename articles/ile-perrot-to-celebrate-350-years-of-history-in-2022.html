Ile Perrot to celebrate 350 years of history in 2022

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET · Mar 2, 2022

[Before Ile Perrot got its current name, it was called Teionnhonskwaronte, an Iroquois expression meaning "triangle-shaped like the head of a cow." That was back in the 1640s, when the newly-founded city of Montreal was still known as Ville Marie. In 1672, the island was granted to then-governor of Montreal François-Marie Perrot by King Louis XIV of France, creating the seigneury of Ile Perrot.]{.mark}

[From April to October this year, all four municipalities of Ile Perrot will be celebrating the 350th anniversary of the seigneury. The island has a unique history, and no one knows this better than Lise Chartier, the president of the 350th anniversary committee and the island's resident historian.]{.mark}

The author of two history books about the island with another on the way in 2022, Chartier has spent countless hours rummaging through archives to trace the island's history from 1672 to the present day.

Before the seigneury of Vaudreuil was established in 1702, Ile Perrot was the westernmost frontier of inhabited New France. Due to its strategic location between the Ottawa and St. Lawrence rivers, the island was historically used as a waypoint. In Chartier's words, the island was "where people with multiple destinies met: soldiers, slaves, bishops, merchants, millers, farmers, missionaries, innkeepers, lumberjacks, travelers."

The settlement started out as a fur trading post when Perrot delegated his powers to Antoine Lafrenaye de Brucy, who established a trading post in what is now the area around the Galipeault bridge. With all sorts of people passing through, Perrot 's men would trade alcohol and intercept canoes en route to Montreal. In 1676, Perrot granted de Brucy a fief of what is now part of the city of Ile Perrot, which allowed a few settlers such as Jean Lalonde and Robert Henry to move onto the island.

According to Chartier, many of the early settlers dispersed, founding Detroit, exploring the West, going south to Louisiana or as far north as Hudson Bay. But a small number of settlers remained on the island and formed a nucleus whose descendants still live there today. In fact, the aforementioned Jean Lalonde is the direct ancestor of Marc Lalonde, a former Liberal cabinet minister under Pierre-Elliot Trudeau and a member of the Order of Canada. Now 92 years old, Lalonde resides in his ancestral home in Notre Dame de L'Ile Perrot, which was reportedly built in 1736.

Roots run deep on the island, and Chartier hopes to showcase Ile Perrot's long and fascinating history with a series of events planned throughout the spring and summer.

"We had initially planned to begin the celebration with a banquet, but due to COVID, that we've moved that to October. Instead, on April 20th at noon, church bells will go off across the island for five minutes to mark the start of the festivities," Chartier said.

Some of the highlights of the festivities include poetry and dance evenings, historical reenactments from both European and Indigenous perspectives, guided historical bus tours, and "Where is Perrot?"---a game that challenges the public to locate 100 handmade ceramic François-Marie Perrot statuettes hidden in the parks across the island.

The event schedule has not been officially announced yet, but Chartier encourages those interested to subscribe to the Facebook page "350e de l 'île Perrot" for updates and fun historical facts.
