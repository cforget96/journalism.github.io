Nature-Action Quebec adds another 18 hectares to Mount Rigaud conservation area

By CHRISTOPHER FORGET The 1019 Report · Feb 2, 2022

In an effort to conserve part of the largest forest in the region, Nature-Action Quebec [announced on January 18 that it had]{.mark} acquired another 18 hectares of land around Mount Rigaud, bringing its land holdings in the area to over 200 hectares. And the non-profit environmental conservation group says it might still be buying more land on the mountain.

"It is always possible we will acquire more land. It is one of the NAQ's goals to create corridors of forest that connect our properties," said Mégane Barlet, an NAQ project manager who worked on the Mount Rigaud territory acquisition. "We have worked with the town of Rigaud, the Vaudreuil-Soulanges MRC, and private landowners to promote the conservation of natural spaces in the area."

The NAQ has been committed to protecting natural spaces and fighting climate change for more than 35 years. It began acquiring land across the province in 2007, purchasing about 1,100 hectares since then. It has also helped municipalities protect around 2,000 hectares of natural spaces from development.

The organization says the Mount Rigaud area, which encompasses 4,300 hectares,

is particularly rich in ecological value and biodiversity. The area is home to a number of endangered plants and animals, including the butternut tree, the Canada warbler and the ringneck snake.

In a statement issued in support of the latest land purchase in Rigaud federal Environment Minister Steven Guilbeault praised the move.

"This project will contribute to the protection of Canada's biodiversity and recovery of species at risk," Guilbeault said. "Much remains to be done, but we are making significant progress towards achieving our objective of protecting a quarter of lands and oceans by 2025, and we are taking the necessary steps to achieve 30 per cent by 2030."

In the fall of 2020, a private individual sparked public outrage when it became known he had purchased 42 acres of land on what locals refer to as Mount Rigaud's "second peak" to build a home. A petition with nearly 15,000 signatures called for all levels of government to put an end to the project, which the petition considered to be unsustainable. The building project was approved by the town of Rigaud and regional officials. The controversy has since sparked the landowner to pledge to acquire and protect an area totaling one-and-a-half times his current lot.

Barlet stated that the NAQ would not take a position on the matter. She did add, however, that the organization often works closely with private landowners to advise them on conservation.
